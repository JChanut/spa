According to European Law:

The aim of non-discrimination law is to allow all individuals an equal and fair prospect to access opportunities available in a society. We make choices on a daily basis over issues such as with whom we socialise, where we shop and where we work. We prefer certain things and certain people over others. While expressing our subjective preferences is commonplace and normal, at times we may exercise functions that place us in a position of authority or allow us to take decisions that may have a direct impact on others' lives. We may be civil servants, shopkeepers, employers, landlords or doctors who decide over how public powers are used, or how private goods and services are offered. In these non-personal contexts, nondiscrimination law intervenes in the choices we make in two ways: direct and indirect discrimination.

Direct discrimination will have occurred when three conditions are filled in a case:

Condition 1): an individual is treated unfavorably...
Condition 2): by comparison to how others, who are in a similar situation, have been or would be treated...
Condition 3): and the reason for this is a particular characteristic they hold which falls under a 'protected ground' (It can include 'discrimination by association', where the victim of the discrimination is not the person with the protected characteristic).

The range of 'protected grounds' that exist in European nondiscrimination law are: sex, sexual orientation, disability, age, race, ethnic origin, national origin and religion or belief.

Indirect discrimination will have occurred when three conditions are filled in a case:

Condition 1): a neutral rule, criterion or practice
Condition 2): that affects a group
Condition 3): in a significantly more negative way by comparison to others in a similar situation.

  • Condition 1): a neutral rule, criterion or practice
  • Condition 2): that affects a group
  • Condition 3): in a significantly more negative way by comparison to others in a similar situation.
